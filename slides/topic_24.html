Topic 24—Set proofs

Demirbaş & Rechnitzer

Proving things

Writing set operations as statements

Let \(A,B\) be sets.

Subset and equality

  • \((A \subseteq B) \quad \equiv \quad (\forall x\in A, x\in B) \quad \equiv \quad( x\in A \implies x\in B)\text{.}\)
  • \(\displaystyle (A = B) \quad \equiv \quad \left( (A \subseteq B) \land (B \subseteq A) \right) \quad \equiv \quad \left((x \in A) \iff (x \in B) \right)\)

Intersection and union

  • \(\displaystyle (x \in A \cap B) \quad \equiv \quad \left( x\in A \land x\in B \right)\)
  • \(\displaystyle (x \in A \cup B) \quad \equiv \quad \left( x\in A \lor x\in B \right)\)

Complement and difference

  • \(\displaystyle (x \in \bar{A}) \quad \equiv \quad (x \not\in A) \quad \equiv \quad \neg(x \in A)\)
  • \(\displaystyle (x \in A - B) \quad \equiv \quad \left( (x \in A) \land (x \not\in B)\right) \quad \equiv \quad \left((x \in A) \land \neg(x \in B) \right)\)

A subset example

Proposition:

Let \(A = \set{n \in \mathbb{Z} \st 6\mid n}\) and \(B = \set{n \in \mathbb{Z} \st 2\mid n}\text{,}\) then \(A \subseteq B\)

Scratch work

  • We need to prove \(a \in A \implies a \in B\)
  • So assume that \(a \in A\text{.}\) Hence \(a\) is an integer divisible by 6
  • This means \(a = 6k\) for some \(k \in \mathbb{Z}\text{.}\)
  • We need to show that \(a \in B\) which means we need to show that \(2 \mid a\)
  • But since \(a = 6k\text{,}\) we know \(a=2 \cdot 3k\) so, \(2 \mid a\) as required.

Write it up nicely

\(A =\set{n \in \mathbb{Z} \st 6 \mid n} \subseteq \set{n \in \mathbb{Z} \st 2 \mid n} = B\)

Proof.

  • Let the sets \(A,B\) be as stated and assume that \(a \in A\text{.}\)
  • Hence we know that \(6 \mid a\) and so \(a = 6k\)
  • This implies that \(a = 2 (3k)\) and so \(2 \mid a\)
  • By the definition of the set \(B\text{,}\) \(a \in B\)
  • So \(A \subseteq B\) as required

Another example

Proposition:

Let \(A,B,C\) be sets. If \(A\subseteq B\) and \(B \subseteq C\) then \(A \subseteq C\text{.}\)

Scratch work

  • What do we assume? That \(A\subseteq B\) and \(B \subseteq C\text{.}\)

  • What do we need to prove? \(A \subseteq C\text{,}\) that is \(\qquad (x \in A) \implies (x \in C)\)

  • A problem — what do we assume? Either \(x \in A\) or \(x \not \in A\text{.}\)

    • If \(x \in A\) then since \(A \subseteq B\text{,}\) \(x \in B\text{.}\)

      Then since \(x \in B\) and \(B \subseteq C\text{,}\) \(x \in C\)

    • If \(x \not\in A\) then the implication “\((x \in A) \implies (x \in C)\)” is true.

Write it up

\((A \subseteq B) \land (B \subseteq C) \implies (A \subseteq C)\)

Proof.

  • Assume that \(A \subseteq B\) and \(B \subseteq C\text{.}\)
  • Further let \(x \in A\)
  • Since \(A \subseteq B\text{,}\) we know that \(x \in B\)
  • Then similarly, since \(B \subseteq C\text{,}\) we know that \(x \in C\)
  • Hence \(A \subseteq C\) as required

A distributive result

Proposition:

Let \(A,B,C\) be sets, then \(A \cup (B\cap C) = (A\cup C) \cap (A\cup B)\text{.}\)

Scratch work

Just do one inclusion

\(A \cup (B\cap C) \subseteq (A\cup C) \cap (A\cup B)\)

  • We have to prove LHS is a subset of RHS
  • Let \(x \in \) LHS. Hence \(x \in A\) or \(x \in B \cap C\text{.}\)

  • So we have 2 cases to consider

    • Assume \(x \in A\text{.}\) Then \(x \in A \cup C\) and \(x \in A \cup B\)

    • Now assume \(x \in B \cap C\text{,}\) then \(x \in B\) and \(x \in C\)

      Since \(x \in B\text{,}\) we know \(x \in B \cup A\text{.}\)

      Similarly \(x \in C\text{,}\) so \(x \in C \cup A\text{.}\)

  • In both cases, \(x \in A\cup B\) and \(x \in A \cup C\text{,}\) so \(x \in \) RHS as required

Write it up

\(A \cup (B\cap C) \subseteq (A\cup C) \cap (A\cup B)\)

Proof.

Let \(x \in A \cup (B\cap C)\text{,}\) so that \(x \in A\) or \(x \in B\cap C\text{.}\) We consider each case separately.

  • Assume that \(x \in A\text{,}\) then we know that \(x \in A \cup B\text{.}\) Simiarly, we have \(x \in A \cup C\text{.}\)

  • Now assume that \(x \in B\cap C\text{,}\) so that \(x \in B\) and \(x \in C\text{.}\)

    Since \(x \in B\) it follows that \(x \in B \cup A\text{.}\) Similarly, because \(x \in C\text{,}\) \(x \in C \cup A\text{.}\)

In both cases, \(x \in (A \cup B)\) and \(x \in (A \cup C)\text{.}\) Hence \(x \in (A\cup C) \cap (A\cup B)\) as required.
