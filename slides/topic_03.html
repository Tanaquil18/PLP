Topic 3 — And, or & not

Demirbaş & Rechnitzer

And, or & not

Negation

Given \(P\) we can form a new statement with the opposite truth value.

It is not the case that \(P\)

Definition:

The negation of a statement \(P\) is denoted \(\neg P\text{.}\)

  • When \(P\) is true, the negation \(\neg P\) is false.
  • When \(P\) is false, the negation \(\neg P\) is true.

The negation is also denoted \(!P\) and \(\lnot P\text{.}\)

Examples and a table

  • The negation of “It is tuesday” is “It is not Tuesday”
  • The negation of “\(4 \in A\)” is “\(4 \not\in A\)”
  • The negation of “4 is even” is “4 is not even” or better “4 is odd”.

We can summarise what negation does to truth values via a truth table

\(P\) \(\neg P\) \(\neg(\neg P)\)
T F T
F T F

Note

  • the double negation of \(P\) has the same truth value as \(P\)
  • the law of the excluded middle: exactly one of \(P\) or \((\neg P)\) is true.

Conjunction, and, disjunction, & or

We combine statements using and & or to make new statements.

The words “and”, “or” have precise mathematical meanings

Definition:

Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be statements.

  • The disjunction of \(P\) and \(Q\) is “\(P\) or \(Q\)” and is denoted \(P \lor Q\text{.}\)

    \(P\lor Q\) is true when at least one of \(P,Q\) is true, else false.

  • The conjunction of \(P\) and \(Q\) is “\(P\) and \(Q\)” and is denoted \(P \land Q\text{.}\)

    \(P\land Q\) is true when both \(P,Q\) are true, else false.

Note: colloquial use of “or” is often different from this mathematical “or”

Examples and tables

Let \(P\) be “8 is even” and let \(Q\) be “15 is prime”, then

  • \(P \lor Q\) is “8 is even or 15 is prime”
  • \(P \land Q\) is “8 is even and 15 is prime”
The first is true since \(P\) is true, the second is false since \(Q\) is false.

A truth table helps summarise:

\(P\) \(Q\) \(P \lor Q\) \(P \land Q\)
T T T T
T F T F
F T T F
F F F F

Inclusive and exclusive

Mathematical “or”or is inclusive — \(P \lor Q\) is true when at least one statement is true.

Colloquial “or” is often exclusive — \(P \;\mathrm{xor}\; Q\) is true when exactly one statement is true.

Would you like chicken or beef for dinner?

\(P\) \(Q\) \(P \lor Q\) \(P \; \mathrm{xor}\; Q\)
T T T F
T F T T
F T T T
F F F F

For exclusive-or write “Exactly one of \(P\) or \(Q\)” or “\(P\) or \(Q\) but not both”.
