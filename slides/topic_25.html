Cartesian and power set proofs

A Cartesian example

Proposition:

Let \(A,B,C\) be sets, then \(A \times (B\cup C) = (A\times B) \cup (A\times C)\text{.}\)

Scratch work

  • We'll start with \(A \times (B \cup C) \supseteq (A\times B) \cup (A\times C) \)
  • Since cartesian product, assume \((x,y) \in \) RHS

  • So \((x,y) \in A \times B\) or \((x,y) \in A \times C\)

    • Case 1: When \((x,y) \in A \times B\text{,}\) \(x\in A\) and \(y \in B\text{.}\) Hence \(y \in B \cup C\)
    • Case 2: When \((x,y) \in A \times C\text{,}\) \(x\in A\) and \(y \in C\text{.}\) Hence \(y \in C \cup B\)

  • In both cases, \(x \in A\) and \(y \in B\cup C\text{,}\) so \((x,y) \in \) LHS.

Continuing Cartesian example

\(A \times (B\cup C) = (A\times B) \cup (A\times C)\)

Scratch work continued

  • No do \(A \times (B \cup C) \subseteq (A\times B) \cup (A\times C) \)
  • Assume \((x,y) \in \) LHS

  • Then \(x \in A\) and \(y \in B \cup C\) , so \(y \in B\) or \(y \in C\)

    • Case 1: When \(y \in B\text{,}\) we know that \((x,y) \in A \times B\text{,}\) so \((x,y) \in (A\times B) \cup (A \times C)\)
    • Case 2: When \(y \in C\text{,}\) we know that \((x,y) \in A \times C\text{,}\) so \((x,y) \in (A\times C) \cup (A \times B)\)

  • In both cases, \((x,y) \in \) RHS

You can write this up nicely.

Power set warm-up

Proposition:

\begin{align*} X \subseteq A \amp \implies X \subseteq A \cup B\\ X \subseteq A \cap B \amp \implies X \subseteq A \\ (X \subseteq A) \land (X \subseteq B) \amp \iff X \subseteq A \cap B \end{align*}

Proof.

  • Let \(X \subseteq A\text{.}\) Assume that \(x \in X\text{,}\) which implies that \(x \in A\text{.}\) Hence \(x \in A \cup B\text{.}\)
  • Now let \(X \subseteq A \cap B\) and assume \(x \in X\text{.}\) Hence \(x \in A \cap B\) and so \(x \in A\text{.}\)
  • Let \(X \subseteq A\) and \(X \subseteq B\text{,}\) and assume \(x \in X\text{.}\) Then \(x \in A\) and \(x \in B\text{,}\) so \(x \in A \cap B\text{.}\)
  • Finally let \(X \subseteq A \cap B\text{.}\) Since \(A\cap B \subseteq A\) we know \(X \subseteq A\text{.}\) Similar reasoning gives \(X \subseteq B\text{.}\)

A power set example

Proposition:

Let \(A,B\) be sets then \(\pow{A}\cup\pow{B} \subseteq \pow{A\cup B}\text{.}\) The reverse inclusion does not hold.

Scratch work

  • There are two things to prove here. Start with the inclusion.
  • Is equivalent to \(X \in \pow{A}\cup\pow{B} \implies X \in \pow{A\cup B}\text{.}\)
  • So let \(X \in \pow{A} \cup \pow{B}\text{.}\) Means \(X\subseteq A\) or \(X \subseteq B\)
  • Now if \(X \subseteq A\) then \(X \subseteq A \cup B\) and so \(X \in \pow{A \cup B}\text{.}\)
  • The case \(X \subseteq B\) will be similar.

Disproof of reverse inclusion

\(\pow{A\cup B} \subseteq \pow{A} \cup \pow{B}\)

Scratch work

  • Since this is really “For all sets \(A,B\) …” so disproof can be counter-example
  • Notice that \(X \in RHS\) then \(X \subseteq A\) or \(X \subseteq B\text{.}\)
  • While a set in \(LHS\) could could contain elements from both \(A,B\)
  • Try to construct very small \(A,B\) to illustrate this.
  • Let \(A=\set{1}, B=\set{2}\text{,}\) then \(\set{1,2} \in LHS\) but not in \(RHS\text{.}\)

Proof of result

Proof.

We first prove the inclusion and then show the reverse inclusion does not hold.

  • Let \(X \in \pow{A} \cup \pow{B}\text{.}\) Hence \(X \subseteq A\) or \(X \subseteq B\text{.}\) If \(X \subseteq A\) then (as shown previously) \(X \subseteq A \cup B\text{.}\) Similarly if \(X \subseteq B\) then \(X \subseteq B \cup A\text{.}\) This implies that \(X \in \pow{A\cup B}\) as required.

  • We disprove the reverse inclusion with a counter example. Let \(A = \set{1}, B = \set{2}\) and \(X = \set{1,2}\text{.}\) Then \(X \in \pow{A\cup B}\text{,}\) however \(X \not \in \pow{A} \cup \pow{B}\text{.}\) Hence \(\pow{A\cup B} \not\subseteq \pow{A}\cup\pow{B}\text{.}\)

Another power set result

Proposition:

Let \(A,B\) be sets, then \(\pow{A}\cap\pow{B} = \pow{A \cap B}\text{.}\)

Scratch work

  • There are two inclusions to prove here.
  • Assume that \(X \in LHS\text{,}\) then \(X \in \pow{A}\) and \(X \in \pow{B}\text{.}\)
  • Hence \(X \subseteq A\) and \(X \subseteq B\) — and we showed this means \(X \subseteq A \cap B\)
  • Thus \(X \in RHS\)
  • Now let \(Y \in RHS\text{,}\) so \(Y \subseteq A \cap B\) — we showed this means \(Y \subseteq A\) and \(Y \subseteq B\)
  • So \(Y \in LHS\) as required.

Write it up nicely

\(\pow{A}\cap\pow{B} = \pow{A \cap B}\)

Proof.

We prove each inclusion in turn.

  • Assume that \(X \in LHS\text{.}\) Then \(X \in \pow{A}\) and \(X \in \pow{B}\text{,}\) and so \(X \subseteq A\) and \(X \subseteq B\text{.}\) Hence \(X \subseteq A \cap B\text{,}\) and thus \(X \in RHS\text{.}\)

  • Now assume that \(Y \in RHS\text{.}\) Then \(Y \in \pow{A \cap B}\) and so \(Y \subseteq A \cap B\text{.}\) This means that \(Y \subseteq A\) and \(Y \subseteq B\text{.}\) Hence \(Y \in \pow{A}\) and \(Y \in \pow{B}\) and thus \(Y \in LHS\text{.}\)
