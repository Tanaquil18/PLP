Topic 17—Disproofs

Demirbaş & Rechnitzer

Disproofs

Disproving a statement

To disprove the statement \(P\text{,}\) we prove that \((\neg P)\) is true.

universal quantifier

Since

\begin{equation*} \neg(\forall x, P(x)) \qquad \equiv \qquad \exists x \st \neg P(x) \end{equation*}
our disproof can be a counter example

existential quantifier

Since

\begin{equation*} \neg(\exists x \st P(x)) \qquad \equiv \qquad \forall x, \neg P(x) \end{equation*}
we have to work harder to show that \((\neg P(x))\) is true for all \(x\text{.}\)

Counter examples do not disprove existential quantifiers

Disprove a universal quantifier

For every \(n \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) \(2^n-1\) or \(2^n+1\) is prime.

scratch work — smells false

  • \(n=1\) gives \(1,3\qquad\) and \(\qquad n=2\) gives \(3,5\)
  • \(n=3\) gives \(7,9\qquad\) and \(\qquad n=4\) gives \(15,17\)
  • \(n=5\) gives \(31, 33\qquad\) and \(\qquad n=6\) gives \(63,65\)

Proof.

Pick \(n=6\text{.}\) Since neither \(2^n-1=63\) or \(2^n+1=65\) are prime, the statement is false.

Our counter-example proves “\(\exists n \in \mathbb{N} \st \text{ neither } 2^n-1, 2^n+1 \text{ are prime}\text{.}\)”

Another example

For all \(a,b,c \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) if \((a \mid bc)\) then \((a \mid c)\) or \((a \mid b)\)

scratch work — again smells false.

  • Since is universal quantifier, a counter example is sufficient
  • Negation is “\(\exists a,b,c \st (a \mid bc) \land (a\nmid b \land a \nmid c)\)”
  • Something about prime-factors feels like the right thing here
  • Pick \(a=4\) and \(b=2, c=2\text{.}\) Then \((4 \mid 2\cdot 2)\) but \(4 \nmid 2\text{.}\)

Proof.

The statement is false. Let \(a=4\) and \(b=c=2\text{.}\) Then \(a \mid bc\) but \(a \nmid b\) and \(a \nmid c\text{.}\)

Disproving an existential quantifier

There exist prime numbers \(p,q\) so that \(p-q=999\)

Typically this is much harder. Sometimes we can reduce to a finite number of cases.

scratch work

  • Since odd-odd = even, we must have that \(q=2\)
  • Then since \(1001 = 7\times 11 \times 13\text{,}\) no such primes exist

Proof.

This is false. Either \(q\) is even or odd.

  • If \(q\) is even, then \(q=2\text{.}\) Since \(999+2 =1001\) is divisible by 7 it is not prime.
  • Now assume that \(q\) is odd. Then we must have that \(q=2k+1\) for some \(k\in \mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) But then \(p=2k+1000\) which is divisible by 2 and so not prime.
Hence no such primes exist.
