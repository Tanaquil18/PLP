Topic 28—Equivalence relations & classes

Demirbaş & Rechnitzer

Equivalence relations

Equivalence relations

Important class of relations are those that are similar to “\(=\)”

Definition:

Let \(R\) be a relation on the set \(A\text{.}\)

We call \(R\) an equivalence relation when it is reflexive, symmetric and transitive.

Examples

  • “has same parity as”
  • “is congruent to”
  • “has same birthday as”
Weaker than equality — can be equivalent without being equal

Pictures

Let \(A = \set{0,1,2,\dots,10}\) and consider congruence modulo 4.

And similarly with “has the same parity as”

Notice that elements of \(A\) fall into connected subsets — equivalence classes

Equivalence classes

Definition:

Let \(R\) be an equivalence relation on \(A\text{.}\)

The equivalence class of \(x \in A\) (with respect to \(R\)) is

\begin{equation*} [x] = \set{a \in A \st a \rel x} \end{equation*}

In our congruent modulo 4 example

\begin{align*} [0]\amp = \set{0,4,8} = [4] = [8] \amp [1] \amp = \set{1,5,9} = [5] = [9]\\ [2] \amp = \set{2,6,10} = [6] = [10] \amp [3] \amp = \set{3,7} = [7] \end{align*}

No equivalence class is empty

Lemma:

Let \(R\) be an equivalence relation on \(A\text{.}\)

For any \(a \in A\text{,}\) \(a \in [a]\)

Proof.

Assume \(R\) is an equivalence relation on \(A\text{,}\) and let \(a \in A\text{.}\)

Since \(R\) is reflexive, we know that \(a \rel a\text{.}\) Hence (by definition), \(a \in [a]\) as required.

Equality of equivalence classes

Theorem:

Suppose \(R\) is an equivalence relation on \(A\text{,}\) and let \(a,b \in A\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} [a]=[b] \iff a \rel b \end{equation*}

Scratch work

  • Have to prove both directions
  • Assume \([a]=[b]\text{,}\) then we need to show \(a \rel b\)
  • We know (from above lemma) that \(a \in [a]\text{,}\) so \(a \in [b]\)
  • Definition of \([b]=\set{x \in A \st x \rel b}\text{,}\) so \(a \rel b\)

Continuing

\([a]=[b] \iff a \rel b\)

Scratch work continued

  • Now assume that \(a \rel b\text{.}\) We need to show \([a] \subseteq [b]\) and \([b]\subseteq [a]\)
  • So let \(x \in [a]\text{,}\) which tells us that \(x \rel a\)

  • We know that \(R\) is transitive, so

    \begin{equation*} (x \rel a) \land (a \rel b) \implies (x \rel b) \end{equation*}
    so \(x \in [b]\)

  • The other inclusion is similar, but we use symmetry of \(R\) to get \(b \rel a\text{.}\)

Proof

Proof.

We prove each implication in turn

  • Assume \(a \rel b\text{.}\) We prove that \([a] \subseteq [b]\) and leave the other inclusion to the reader. Let \(x \in [a]\text{,}\) so that \(x \rel a\text{.}\) Since \(R\) is transitive, and \(a \rel b\text{,}\) we know that \(x \rel b\text{.}\) Hence \(x \in [b]\) as required. The other inclusion is similar, but also uses symmetry of \(R\text{.}\)

  • Now assume that \([a]=[b]\text{.}\) By the lemma above, we know that \(a \in [a]\text{,}\) and so \(a \in [b]\text{.}\) By definition of the equivalence class of \(b\text{,}\) this tells us that \(a \rel b\text{.}\)
