Topic 30—Integers modulo \(n\)

Demirbaş & Rechnitzer

Integers modulo \(n\)

Partition and equivalence classes

  • The equivalence relation “\(\equiv \Mod{n}\)” gives a partition of \(\mathbb{Z}\text{:}\)

    \begin{equation*} \set{[0], [1], [2], \dots, [n-1]} \end{equation*}

  • These equivalence classes are called the integers mod \(n\)
  • They have nice arithmetic properties

Theorem:

Let \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) and let \(a,b \in \set{0,1,\dots,n-1}\text{.}\)

If \(x \in [a]\) and \(y \in [b]\) then

\begin{equation*} x+y \in [a+b] \qquad \text{ and } \qquad x\cdot y \in [a\cdot b] \end{equation*}

Arithmetic modulo \(n\)

\((x \in [a]) \land (y \in [b]) \implies (x+y \in [a+b]) \land (x\cdot y \in [a\cdot b])\)

Scratch work

  • Since \(x \in [a], y \in [b]\) we know that \(n \mid (x-a)\) and \(n \mid (y-b)\text{,}\) so

    \begin{equation*} x = a + nk \qquad \text{ and } \qquad y = b + n \ell \end{equation*}

  • This means that

    \begin{align*} x+y \amp = a + b + n(k+\ell) \amp xy \amp = ab + n(bk+a\ell) + n^2k\ell \end{align*}

  • Which gives

    \begin{equation*} n \mid \left( (x+y)-(a+b)\right) \qquad \text{ and } \qquad n \mid \left(x\cdot y- a \cdot b \right) \end{equation*}

Proof

Proof.

Let \(n,a,b,x,y\) be as stated. Then since \(x \in [a]\) and \(y \in [b]\text{,}\) we know that

\begin{equation*} x = a + nk \qquad \text{and} \qquad y = b + n\ell \qquad \text{ for some } k,\ell \in \mathbb{Z} \end{equation*}
From this we have
\begin{align*} x+y \amp = a + b + n(k+\ell) \amp xy \amp = ab + n(bk+a\ell) + n^2k\ell \end{align*}
and so
\begin{equation*} n \mid \left( (x+y)-(a+b)\right) \qquad \text{ and } \qquad n \mid \left(x\cdot y- a \cdot b \right) \end{equation*}
This shows that \(x+y \in [a+b]\) and \(x\cdot y \in [a\cdot b]\) as required.

Modular arithmetic

Definition:

Let \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) and consider the equivalence classes of congruence modulo \(n\text{.}\)

The integers modulo \(n\) is the set

\begin{equation*} \mathbb{Z}_n = \set{[0], [1], [2], \dots, [n-1]} \end{equation*}
The elements of \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) can be added and multiplied by the rule
\begin{equation*} [a]+[b] = [a+b] \qquad [a]\cdot [b] = [a\cdot b] \end{equation*}
